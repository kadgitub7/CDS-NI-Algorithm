# Comparative Algorithmic Analysis: CDS-OVR vs. Benchmark Methods on the UCI Arrhythmia Dataset

## 1. Introduction and Scope

This document provides a rigorous, mechanism-level analysis of the CDS-OVR (Class-Directed Splitting with One-vs-Rest decomposition) classifier against six benchmark studies and the original CDS algorithm on the UCI Arrhythmia dataset. Rather than merely reporting accuracy differences, this analysis traces each performance gap to specific algorithmic design decisions, dataset properties, and their interactions. Every claim is grounded in either (a) the published methodology and results of the benchmark paper, (b) the CDS-OVR source code (`cds_ovr.py`, W11-01 configuration), or (c) verifiable properties of the UCI Arrhythmia dataset.

### 1.2 Statistical Context for All Comparisons

A methodological observation underlies every comparison in this document: **CDS-OVR is the only method that reports variance across multiple random seeds.** All six benchmark papers report single-split or single-run results without confidence intervals. On a dataset of 452 patients (or 416 after class removal), small test sets (42 patients at 90/10) produce high variance — CDS-OVR's own 90/10 results range from 76.2% to 92.9% across 10 seeds (a 16.7 pp spread). Any single-split result from any method falls somewhere in an unreported distribution. This does not invalidate the benchmark results, but it means that accuracy differences smaller than approximately ±5 pp on 90/10 splits cannot be confidently attributed to algorithmic superiority without further evidence.

The full split-ratio sweep confirms a monotonic trend: CDS-OVR mean multiclass accuracy rises from 79.4% (50/50) to 86.2% (90/10), a +6.8 pp improvement driven by increased per-class training data enabling better posterior estimates. Variance also increases with smaller test sets: σ = 2.85% at 50/50 (N=208) vs. σ = 4.97% at 90/10 (N=42). The 10-fold CV result (84.7% mean, σ=0.93%) is the most stable estimate because all 416 patients are tested.

This analysis therefore focuses on identifying **structural** reasons for performance differences — mechanisms that would produce consistent advantages or disadvantages across multiple splits — rather than attributing small numerical differences to algorithmic design.

## 2. Dataset Properties That Determine Classifier Performance

The UCI Arrhythmia dataset has six structural properties that interact with classifier design choices to produce the observed accuracy differences. Understanding these properties is prerequisite to understanding why any classifier achieves its reported numbers.

### 2.1 Class Distribution

After CDS-OVR's removal of classes with insufficient representation (original UCI classes 7, 8, 14, 15, 16 — internally remapped to 7, 8, 11, 12, 13 via `cds_ovr.py` line 61 — totaling 36 patients across 5 classes: class 7 has 3, class 8 has 2, class 14 has 4, class 15 has 5, class 16/Other has 22 patients), the working dataset contains 416 patients across 8 classes:

| Class | Condition | n | % | n_train (90/10) | n_train (60/40) |
|-------|-----------|---|---|----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Normal | 245 | 58.9% | ~220 | ~147 |
| 2 | Ischemic Changes (CAD) | 44 | 10.6% | ~40 | ~26 |
| 3 | Old Anterior MI | 15 | 3.6% | ~14 | ~9 |
| 4 | Old Inferior MI | 15 | 3.6% | ~14 | ~9 |
| 5 | Sinus Tachycardia | 13 | 3.1% | ~12 | ~8 |
| 6 | Sinus Bradycardia | 25 | 6.0% | ~23 | ~15 |
| 9 | Left Bundle Branch Block | 9 | 2.2% | ~8 | ~5 |
| 10 | Right Bundle Branch Block | 50 | 12.0% | ~45 | ~30 |

The imbalance ratio between the largest class (Normal, 245 patients) and smallest class (LBBB, 9 patients) is 27.2:1. This ratio is the single most important dataset property affecting classifier design. Methods that treat all classes uniformly (identical hyperparameters, shared feature sets, coupled probability outputs) will systematically underperform on the rare disease classes (classes 4, 5, 9 with 15, 13, 9 patients respectively, and class 3 with 15 patients), which collectively contain 52 patients (12.5% of the dataset) but represent 4 of 7 disease categories.

### 2.2 Feature Structure

The 279 features comprise:
- **Demographic features** (4): age, sex, height, weight
- **Continuous ECG measurements** (~206): intervals, durations, amplitudes, axes across 12 leads
- **Binary morphological indicators** (~69): presence/absence of specific waveform features

Three properties of this feature structure directly affect classifier accuracy:

**Inter-feature correlation.** ECG measurements from different leads of the same waveform component are strongly correlated (e.g., P-wave amplitude across leads). Among Sharma's 106 selected features alone, 32 feature pairs exceed |r| > 0.8 and 2 pairs exceed |r| > 0.9 (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 3). Any feature selection method that treats features independently will retain redundant features, consuming model capacity without adding discriminative information.

**Concentrated missing values.** The overall missing rate is low (~0.3%), but it is concentrated in specific features — column 14 alone exceeds 80% missing (Sharma 2024, Section 3.2). Missing values are not randomly distributed across patients; they tend to occur in features that are only measured when clinical abnormalities are present. This means missing values carry diagnostic information: a feature measured as present may itself be a disease indicator. Methods that delete columns with high missing rates (Islam 2023: columns with >50% missing; Sharma 2024: column 14) discard exactly the features most likely to distinguish specific disease classes.

**Sex-dependent distributions.** Feature 1 (sex) is binary (179 male, 237 female) and affects the distributions of ECG parameters. Four features show medium-or-larger sex effects (Cohen's d > 0.5): feature 4 (d=0.677, male mean=94.25 vs. female mean=84.26), feature 190 (d=0.536), feature 252 (d=0.520), and feature 3 (d=0.511) (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 8). Class 3 (Old Anterior MI) is 100% male (15/15 patients), making sex a near-perfect class-3 predictor. Methods that do not account for sex-dependent variation must learn more complex decision boundaries.

### 2.3 Interaction Between Dataset Properties and Classifier Design

| Dataset Property | Classifier Design Vulnerability | Affected Benchmarks |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| 61:1 class imbalance | Uniform hyperparameters suppress rare classes | All six benchmarks |
| 279 features, 416 patients | Curse of dimensionality degrades distance-based methods | Islam (SVM/RF), Jadhav (MLP without FS) |
| Correlated features | Redundant feature selection wastes model capacity | Mustaqeem (94 features), Sharma (101 features) |
| Concentrated missing values | Column deletion removes discriminative features | Islam (279→252), Sharma (dropped column 14 + rows) |
| Sex-dependent distributions | Combined population requires more complex boundaries | All benchmarks except CDS-OVR |
| Small disease classes | Pairwise classifiers / SoftMax coupling disadvantage rare classes | Mustaqeem (OAO), Irfan (SoftMax), Sharma (RF global) |

## 3. The Original CDS Algorithm: Identified Failure Modes

The original CDS (`cds.py`) was a binary classifier producing HEALTHY, UNHEALTHY, or SCREENING predictions. Each design limitation of the original CDS motivated a specific innovation in CDS-OVR. This section traces those limitations to code-level mechanisms.

### 3.1 Hard Healthy Range — Catastrophic False Positive Generation

The original CDS computed per-feature "healthy ranges" as [min(healthy_values), max(healthy_values)] during training (`cds.py`, lines 238–248). During prediction, any single feature value outside this range triggered an immediate UNHEALTHY return (`cds.py`, lines 360–361), bypassing all subsequent evidence accumulation.

This design produces a systematic false positive bias. Consider: with 279 features, each having an independently estimated healthy range from the training set, a test patient has 279 independent opportunities to fall outside at least one range. If the probability of a healthy test patient exceeding the training range on any single feature is p (even as low as 0.01), the probability of at least one exceedance is:

P(≥1 exceedance) = 1 − (1 − p)^279

For p = 0.01: P = 1 − 0.99^279 ≈ 0.94
For p = 0.005: P = 1 − 0.995^279 ≈ 0.75

Even with a per-feature exceedance rate as low as 0.5%, three-quarters of healthy test patients would be incorrectly flagged. The probability p is not negligible because the training-set range is a sample statistic with variance inversely proportional to sample size — extreme quantiles of any distribution are poorly estimated from finite samples.

**CDS-OVR's resolution**: The healthy bar mechanism (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 455–468) replaces the binary hard-range test with a continuous healthy score that interacts with per-class disease thresholds. No single feature can trigger an immediate classification. The healthy score is computed from the same dual-AF evidence accumulation pipeline as disease scores, integrating evidence across all retained features before making a decision.

### 3.2 Unsupervised Equal-Width Binning

The original CDS used Sturges' rule (K = ⌈1 + log₂(n)⌉) to determine bin count and equal-width intervals. This binning is entirely independent of the class labels — it partitions the feature range uniformly regardless of where class boundaries occur. If a clinically significant boundary (e.g., heart rate = 100 bpm separating normal from tachycardic) falls within the interior of a bin, the model cannot detect it.

**CDS-OVR's resolution**: Supervised chi-squared binning (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 209–265) places split points where class composition changes most significantly. Each split maximizes the chi-squared statistic between the target class and all other classes, ensuring that bin boundaries align with actual class boundaries in the data.

### 3.3 Single-Accumulator Evidence and No Multiclass Capability

The original CDS accumulated a single accumulation factor (AF) that could only increase, and produced binary predictions (HEALTHY/UNHEALTHY) without identifying which disease was present. Evidence against a disease hypothesis was structurally invisible — it simply was not accumulated.

**CDS-OVR's resolution**: The One-vs-Rest decomposition trains independent models for each of 8 classes, and the dual-AF mechanism (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 399–442) separately tracks AF_for and AF_against, combining them via ratio scoring: score = (AF_for + ε) / (AF_against + ε). This enables the model to distinguish between strong disease evidence, ambiguous evidence, and strong healthy evidence.

## 4. CDS-OVR Architecture: Design Decisions and Their Effects

This section describes each component of CDS-OVR with reference to the source code, explains the design rationale, and traces its effect on classification accuracy.

### 4.1 One-vs-Rest Decomposition with Independent Per-Class Models

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 370–394): For each of the 8 classes, a separate model is trained by computing `is_target = (nd_labels == target_class)` and running supervised binning, Fisher scoring, and feature selection against this binary partition. During prediction (lines 445–470), each class model produces an independent score, and the highest-scoring disease class exceeding its threshold is selected.

**Design rationale**: OVR with independent per-class pipelines ensures that the feature set, bin boundaries, and decision thresholds for each disease are optimized for that disease alone. This is the central architectural distinction from all benchmarks: **class-awareness at every pipeline stage**, not just at the final classification layer.

**Contrast with coupled architectures**: Mustaqeem's One-Against-One (OAO) SVM creates C(C,2) = 78 pairwise classifiers for the 13 non-empty classes (the dataset has 16 class labels but 3 have zero patients). Each pairwise classifier for rare class k vs. abundant class 1 sees extreme imbalance (e.g., 2 vs. 245 patients for class 8 vs. class 1), and the SVM's margin-maximizing objective pushes the hyperplane close to the rare class, producing biased vote counts. Irfan's CNN-LSTM uses SoftMax output, which enforces Σ P(class_i) = 1 — any increase in the logit for the majority class mechanically suppresses probabilities for all minority classes, even when disease evidence is strong.

CDS-OVR's per-class scores are computed from separate models with no mathematical coupling. The class 2 score of 4.5 and the class 10 score of 6.2 are derived from entirely different feature sets, bin boundaries, and posterior estimates. The only inter-class interaction is through the healthy bar mechanism, which is designed to prevent false positives by raising disease thresholds when healthy evidence is strong.

### 4.2 Supervised Chi-Squared Binning

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 209–265): For each continuous feature in each class model, the algorithm iteratively selects the split point maximizing the Pearson chi-squared statistic between the target-class indicator and the resulting bin assignment. Constraints prevent overfitting: each side of a split must contain ≥ MIN_SUPPORT patients, the chi-squared gain must exceed 0.5, and at most MAX_BINS = 6 bins are created.

**Effect on evidence quality**: Supervised bin boundaries produce bins with more homogeneous class composition than equal-width bins. **Numerical evidence** (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 9): comparing supervised chi-squared bins vs. equal-width (Sturges) bins on the same features, supervised binning produces stronger posterior shifts for class 6 (2.5× stronger average |posterior shift|: 0.253 vs. 0.102) and class 10 (1.1× stronger: 0.482 vs. 0.441). For class 6's best feature (feature 14, FDR=2.526), supervised binning creates a bin with posterior = 0.754 (12/15 target patients concentrated) vs. a prior of 6.0% — a 69.4 pp shift. Equal-width bins spread these patients across more bins, diluting the signal. Within a supervised bin, the Laplace-smoothed posterior `p_class[b] = (target_counts[b] + α · prior) / (bin_counts[b] + α)` is a more accurate estimate of the true class probability, producing more decisive evidence signals.

**Regularization against overfitting**: The chi-squared gain threshold of 0.5 prevents the creation of bins driven by sampling noise rather than genuine class structure. The MAX_BINS = 6 cap limits model complexity per feature. Combined with the prediction-time bin count filter (`bc < 3 → skip`), these constraints ensure that evidence is only accumulated from bins with sufficient statistical support.

### 4.3 Dual Accumulation Factor with Ratio Scoring

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 399–442): For each retained feature in a class model, the patient's value is binned, the posterior shift `p_class[b] − prior` is computed, weighted by confidence (min(1, bin_count/10)) and Fisher weight (√(fisher/max_fisher), floored at 0.1), and added to either AF_for (positive shift) or AF_against × against_scale (negative shift). The final score is (AF_for + 0.1) / (AF_against + 0.1).

**Why ratio scoring is more informative than single-accumulator methods**: A single accumulator that only increases (original CDS) cannot represent the case where 5 features provide evidence against a disease and 3 features provide evidence for it — the accumulator simply reaches some intermediate value, indistinguishable from 3 features providing moderate for-evidence and 0 against.

The ratio score creates an interpretable scale:
- score ≈ 1.0: balanced for/against evidence — ambiguous
- score >> 1.0: dominant for-evidence — likely disease
- score << 1.0: dominant against-evidence — likely not disease

**Numerical evidence** (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 10): In 10-fold CV (seed=13), correctly classified patients (361/416) have mean ratio = 6.859 (median = 4.545), while incorrectly classified patients (55/416) have mean ratio = 1.664 (median = 1.247). The separation is stark: correct predictions show AF_for >> AF_against (mean AF_for = 1.389 vs. AF_against = 0.241), while wrong predictions show balanced accumulation (mean AF_for = 0.505 vs. AF_against = 0.565). A single accumulator cannot distinguish these two states — it would show moderate values in both cases.

The epsilon term (ε = 0.1) is critical: it prevents division by zero and ensures that AF_for must exceed approximately ε × threshold ≈ 0.3 to trigger classification, requiring multiple concordant feature signals rather than a single feature with strong but potentially unreliable evidence.

### 4.4 Fisher Discriminant Ratio Feature Weighting

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 316–321, applied at line 430): For each feature in each class model, the Fisher discriminant ratio FDR = (μ_target − μ_rest)² / (σ²_target + σ²_rest) is computed. During evidence accumulation, each feature's contribution is scaled by fw = max(√(FDR_f / max_FDR), 0.1).

**Design rationale**: Not all features contribute equally to class discrimination. A feature where the target class has a distinct mean (large numerator) and tight distribution (small denominator) is more informative than a feature with overlapping distributions. Fisher weighting ensures that the evidence score is dominated by the most discriminative features for each specific class.

**Per-class specificity**: Because FDR is computed separately for each class model, a feature can receive high weight for one class and low weight for another. Heart rate has high FDR for class 6 (sinus bradycardia — defined by low heart rate) but low FDR for class 3 (anterior MI — heart rate is typically normal). This per-class weighting is structurally impossible in methods that use a single global feature set (Sharma, Islam, Hybrid FS) or global feature importance (Mustaqeem's RF-based wrapper).

### 4.5 Correlation-Based Feature Filtering

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 330–367): For each class model, the top 3 × FPC (54) features ranked by action score are considered. Features are greedily selected subject to the constraint that no selected feature has |correlation| > 0.8 with any previously selected feature, up to FPC = 18 features per class.

**Effect**: This prevents the model from spending multiple feature slots on redundant measurements. In ECG data, where the same waveform component is measured across 12 leads, this is particularly important. Without correlation filtering, a class 10 (RBBB) model might select QRS duration measurements from 5 correlated leads, all telling the same story, wasting 4 slots that could detect T-wave inversion, axis deviation, or R-wave amplitude patterns.

**Contrast with Sharma's Pearson filtering**: Sharma 2024 selects features whose Pearson correlation with the target variable exceeds 0.1, retaining 101 of 279 features. This threshold is very low — features with |r| = 0.1 explain only 1% of the variance in the target. More importantly, this is a single global feature set applied to the RF classifier. Sharma does not report removing inter-feature correlation (their heatmap in Figure 3 shows substantial residual correlation among the 101 selected features). CDS-OVR's approach selects fewer features (18 per class) but ensures they are both discriminative (ranked by chi-squared score and FDR) and non-redundant (pairwise |r| < 0.8).

### 4.6 Per-Class Imbalance Adaptation

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 42–44):

| Parameter | Rare classes {4, 5, 9} | Common classes |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| MIN_SUPPORT (training) | 2 | 3 |
| CONF_SUPPORT (training) | 5 | 10 |
| against_scale (prediction) | 0.5 | 0.8 |

During prediction, all classes use hardcoded MIN_SUPPORT = 3 and CONF_SUPPORT = 10 (lines 425–429).

**Rationale for the training/prediction asymmetry**: Training with MIN_SUPPORT = 2 allows the discovery of potentially discriminative features for rare classes — features where even 2 patients concentrate in the same bin. Prediction with MIN_SUPPORT = 3 ensures that evidence is only accumulated from bins with sufficient sample support, preventing unreliable posterior estimates from driving the ratio score.

**against_scale = 0.5 for rare classes**: This is the most distinctive imbalance handling mechanism in CDS-OVR. It encodes a principled asymmetry: with only 9–15 patients in the rare classes {4, 5, 9}, the model's estimate of "what a non-class-k patient looks like" is based on ~400 patients from 7 other classes, which is a diverse population with high variance. A feature bin showing negative evidence (p_class < prior) could be negative simply because the bin contains a mix of other disease classes, not because the target class is absent. Downweighting against-evidence by 50% reflects this greater uncertainty in the meaning of negative evidence for rare classes.

**No benchmark implements per-class parametrization.** Sharma's RF uses the same `min_samples_split = 2` and `gini` criterion for all trees; Mustaqeem uses the same SVM kernel and regularization for all 78 pairwise classifiers; Irfan uses the same CNN-LSTM architecture and SoftMax for all 13 classes.

### 4.7 Dynamic Healthy Bar and Suspicion Mechanism

**Implementation** (`cds_ovr.py`, lines 455–468):

```
h_score = class_scores[HEALTHY]    # ratio score from the healthy (class 1) model
healthy_bar = min(1.05 × h_score, 5.0)

For each disease class:
  threshold = CLASS_THRESHOLDS[cls]   # e.g., 3.5 for class 2, 3.0 for class 10
  if h_score < 2.0:                  # suspicion mechanism
      threshold -= 0.3
  threshold = max(threshold, healthy_bar)
  if disease_score > threshold → candidate
```

**Design rationale**: The healthy bar creates an adaptive interaction between healthy evidence and disease evidence. **Numerical evidence** (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 13): in 10-fold CV (seed=13), the healthy bar achieves 91.4% specificity (224/245 healthy patients correctly classified) with a disease detection rate of 84.8% (145/171 disease patients identified as having some disease — 137 with the correct class and 8 assigned the wrong disease class). Only 26 disease patients are misclassified as healthy (false negatives), and 21 healthy patients are classified as having a disease (false positives, 8.6% false positive rate). For a patient with strong healthy evidence (h_score = 4.0), the effective threshold becomes max(CLASS_THRESHOLD, 4.2), requiring very strong disease evidence to override. For a patient with weak healthy evidence (h_score = 1.5, triggering suspicion), thresholds are lowered by 0.3, increasing sensitivity to disease detection.

**CLASS_THRESHOLDS[10] = 3.0**: This value was set based on error analysis of earlier model versions. Class 10 (RBBB, 50 patients) showed systematic under-detection. With the current threshold of 3.0, per-class accuracy in 10-fold CV (seed=13, `evidence_analysis.py`, Section 14): class 1: 91.4% (224/245), class 2: 77.3% (34/44), class 3: 93.3% (14/15), class 4: 73.3% (11/15), class 5: 61.5% (8/13), class 6: 84.0% (21/25), class 9: 88.9% (8/9), class 10: 82.0% (41/50). The confusion matrix shows class 10's primary error mode is misclassification to class 1 (8 RBBB patients predicted as Normal), confirming that the healthy bar sometimes overrides disease evidence for borderline RBBB cases.

## 5. Benchmark-by-Benchmark Analysis

### 5.1 Sharma et al. 2024 — Optimized Random Forest

**Published in**: Engineering Research Express, Volume 6, 035209 (2024)

**Method pipeline**:
1. Identify and remove rows with missing values (452 → 420 instances)
2. Drop column 14 (>80% missing values)
3. Pearson correlation-based feature selection: retain features with |r| > 0.1 with target → 101 features (Sharma's reported count; our simulation on the CDS-OVR dataset yields 106, likely differing because Sharma uses all 452 patients including classes 7, 8, 11–13, 16, which shifts correlations — see `evidence_ablation.py`, Section 4)
4. Normalize features
5. Train 6 ML models (LR, XGB, LDA, GNB, SVM, RF), each optimized via Grid Search
6. Evaluate on three splits: 70/30 (DS1), 80/20 (DS2), 90/10 (DS3)
7. Binary classification only (Normal vs. Arrhythmia)

**Best result**: RF on DS3 (90/10): TP=17, FP=0, FN=2, TN=23 → 95.24% accuracy, 100% precision, 89.47% sensitivity, 100% specificity.

**CDS-OVR comparison**: Binary 90/10 best = 97.6% (seed=76: TP=14, FN=1, TN=27, FP=0 — 1 error out of 42 test patients), mean = 88.6% across 10 seeds.

#### 5.1.1 Statistical Fragility of Sharma's 95.24%

Sharma's 90/10 split produces a test set of **42 patients** (23 normal, 19 arrhythmia per their reported confusion matrix: TN=23, TP=17, FN=2, FP=0). The reported 95.24% accuracy corresponds to exactly **2 misclassifications** out of 42. This creates extreme sensitivity to individual patients:

- 2 errors / 42 = 95.24%
- 3 errors / 42 = 92.86%
- 1 error / 42 = 97.62%

A single additional misclassification changes the accuracy by 2.38 pp. Without variance reporting across multiple splits, it is impossible to distinguish whether Sharma's 95.24% represents the method's expected performance or a favorable draw. CDS-OVR's 10-seed evaluation on the same 90/10 protocol shows binary accuracy ranging from 81.0% to 97.6% (σ=4.74%, `run_all_splits.py`), demonstrating that single-split results on 42 test patients are unreliable indicators of true model performance.

#### 5.1.2 Where CDS-OVR Structurally Outperforms Sharma's RF — Feature Selection

Sharma selects 101 features globally using Pearson correlation with a threshold of |r| > 0.1. This approach has three structural weaknesses relative to CDS-OVR:

**Weak threshold retains noise features.** Features with |r| = 0.1 explain only 1% of target variance (r² = 0.01). Numerically: of the 106 features Sharma retains, 42 have r² < 0.02 (explain less than 2% of variance), and 51 fall in the borderline range |r| ∈ [0.1, 0.15] (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 3). Under the null hypothesis of no association, approximately 11.4 features would exceed |r| > 0.1 by chance alone with 420 samples (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 7). These noise features add variance to the RF without improving discrimination.

**Global feature set cannot optimize for individual diseases.** Pearson correlation with the binary target (normal=0, arrhythmia=1) measures each feature's linear association with the combined disease label. A feature that perfectly separates sinus bradycardia from normal (e.g., heart rate < 60) but is irrelevant for all other diseases contributes only 25/207 = 12% of the disease-class variance. Its Pearson |r| with the binary target is diluted by the 182 non-bradycardia disease patients for whom this feature is uninformative. If diluted below 0.1, it is discarded entirely.

CDS-OVR selects 18 features per class using Fisher discriminant ratio, optimized for each disease independently. Across all 8 class models, 109 unique features are used out of a possible 8 × 18 = 144 feature slots (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 4). Pairwise Jaccard overlap between class models ranges from 0.023 (class 3 vs. class 10) to 0.289 (class 4 vs. class 6), confirming that each class model learns a substantially different feature set. Class 10 (RBBB) uses 13 features not shared with any other class model; class 3 (AM) uses 8 unique features. Different diseases get their most discriminative features.

**No inter-feature redundancy removal.** Sharma's Figure 3 (inter-variable correlation matrix) shows substantial correlation among selected features: 32 pairs exceed |r| > 0.8 (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 3). CDS-OVR's CORR_THRESHOLD = 0.8 ensures that within each tree node (sex branch), no two retained features share more than 64% of their variance. When features from both sex branches are pooled per class, the combined set can have higher maximum correlations — up to 0.895 for classes 1, 2, 4, 5 (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 11) — because features filtered independently within each branch may correlate across branches. Within a single branch's 18-feature set, the threshold holds: class 9 (no sex branching effect) achieves max |r| = 0.789, below threshold. The contrast with Sharma remains: Sharma retains 32 highly correlated pairs globally with no filtering mechanism at all.

#### 5.1.3 Where CDS-OVR Structurally Outperforms Sharma's RF — Missing Value Handling

Sharma drops column 14 entirely (>80% missing) and removes rows with remaining missing values (452 → 420, losing 32 patients / 7.1% of the data). This approach has two costs:

**Lost patients are not randomly distributed.** Simulating Sharma's preprocessing (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 2): after dropping column 14, 22 patients still have missing values and are removed. The removal rate is highly uneven: class 2 (CAD) loses 8/44 patients (18.2%), class 3 (AM) loses 2/15 (13.3%), while class 1 (Normal) loses only 8/245 (3.3%). This disproportionate loss depletes minority classes that are already data-starved — class 2 drops from 44 to 36 patients, a 18.2% reduction in an already-small class.

**Column 14 carries diagnostic information.** Feature 13 (0-indexed; Sharma's column 14) has 83.9% missing values, but it is not uninformative — its Fisher discriminant ratio reaches 1.437 for class 3 (Old Anterior MI), ranking it #19 out of 279 features by FDR for that class (`evidence_analysis.py`, Section 12). While not among the top features, it exceeds the FDR of many retained features. CDS-OVR's class 2 model uses feature 13 directly in its retained feature set (evidence report §4). Deleting this feature removes signal that CDS-OVR exploits.

CDS-OVR handles missing values by skipping them at prediction time (`if np.isnan(v): continue` in `_compute_af()`, line 417). No patients are discarded, no features are deleted. When a feature value is available, it contributes to the evidence; when missing, it is simply omitted from the sum. This preserves all 416 patients and all 279 features.

#### 5.1.4 Where CDS-OVR Structurally Outperforms Sharma's RF — Decision Architecture

Random Forest makes predictions by majority vote across an ensemble of decision trees. Each tree partitions the feature space recursively, selecting the best split at each node based on Gini impurity across all classes globally. For binary classification with RF:

- All trees share the same feature space (101 features).
- Each tree samples features randomly at each split (max_features = sqrt, i.e., ~10 features per split).
- The final prediction is the majority vote across all trees.

This architecture has no mechanism to adapt decision sensitivity per disease class. A patient with a rare arrhythmia (e.g., sinus tachycardia affecting 13 patients) will only be correctly classified if enough trees happen to sample the 1–2 features that distinguish tachycardia from normal, and if those features produce splits that survive the Gini criterion. In a binary model, all arrhythmias are lumped together — the RF cannot learn that different diseases require attention to different features.

CDS-OVR's OVR decomposition with per-class feature selection, Fisher weighting, and adaptive thresholds provides exactly this disease-specific attention. The binary accuracy achieved by CDS-OVR (97.6% best) is a byproduct of its multiclass architecture — it correctly identifies *which* disease each patient has, and binary accuracy follows from the fact that correctly classified disease patients are also correctly classified as "not normal."

#### 5.1.5 Where Sharma's RF May Have Advantages

**Feature interactions via tree ensembles.** RF captures conjunctive patterns through path depth: a tree can split on QRS duration > 120 ms at one node and right axis > 90° at a child node, effectively implementing the conjunction "QRS > 120 AND axis > 90°" that indicates RBBB. CDS-OVR evaluates features independently and cannot capture such interactions.

**Implicit dimensionality reduction via bagging.** Each tree sees a bootstrap sample of patients and a random subset of features, providing natural regularization against overfitting. CDS-OVR's regularization operates through different mechanisms (MIN_SUPPORT, chi-squared gain threshold, MAX_BINS cap).

**Zero false positives on this specific split.** Sharma's RF achieved FP = 0 (100% specificity) on their particular 90/10 split. While this may reflect favorable test-set composition, it indicates that RF with careful feature selection can achieve high specificity on this dataset. CDS-OVR's healthy bar mechanism is designed for the same purpose but operates through a different mechanism (continuous threshold scaling rather than tree-based decision rules).

CDS-OVR best 90/10 multiclass: 92.9% vs. Mustaqeem 92.07%. The difference (0.83 pp) is within the variance of single-split evaluation.

CDS-OVR accumulates evidence from each feature independently, treating the conjunction as merely three features with moderate positive evidence rather than one strong combined signal.

**Data augmentation**: Mustaqeem's 5× shadow augmentation (452 → 2,260) increases the effective training size for each pairwise classifier, reducing variance in the support vector estimates and stabilizing the hyperplane positions. CDS-OVR works with the original patient count.

## 6. The 60/40 Performance Gap: A Structural Analysis

CDS-OVR's weakest result (60/40: 80.2% mean multiclass, 86.2% best) contrasts with Irfan's 93.33% on the same protocol. However, CDS-OVR's 60/40 binary accuracy tells a different story: 92.2% best, 84.5% mean. The gap narrows significantly when comparing the same task type:

CDS-OVR's classification pipeline has a hard floor: `if bc < 3: continue` in `_compute_af()`. This means that for any feature-bin pair with fewer than 3 patients in the bin during training, zero evidence is accumulated during prediction.

### 6.2 Why PCA-Based Methods Do Not Have This Problem

PCA computes the covariance matrix from all training patients regardless of class. With 271 training patients at 60/40, the covariance matrix is well-estimated (271 >> 50 components). Every class — including class 9 with only 4 training patients at 60/40 — contributes to and is represented in the PCA transformation. The CNN-LSTM then operates in the 50-dimensional PCA space, where even rare classes can form identifiable clusters.

This is the fundamental tradeoff: **CDS-OVR's class-aware specialization requires sufficient per-class data; PCA-based methods share representation learning across all classes but cannot specialize per-class.**

### 6.3 Potential Resolutions

Several modifications could improve CDS-OVR's 60/40 performance without compromising its 90/10 advantage:

1. **Adaptive bin count**: Reduce MAX_BINS when per-class training data is small (e.g., MAX_BINS = max(2, min(6, n_class_train / 3))).
2. **Lower prediction MIN_SUPPORT for rare classes**: Allow `bc ≥ 2` for rare classes during prediction, accepting higher posterior variance.
3. **Feature-level fallback**: When retained actions are too few, use the single highest-FDR feature with a simple threshold rule instead of binning.

## 7. Honest Assessment: Structural Advantages of Benchmark Methods

### 7.1 Feature Interactions (All Neural/Kernel Methods)

CDS-OVR evaluates features independently. The evidence from feature A is added to the evidence from feature B without considering their joint distribution. This means CDS-OVR cannot detect conjunctive patterns where multiple features must simultaneously meet criteria for a diagnosis. SVM (polynomial kernel), CNN, and RF (tree splits) all capture such interactions through their respective mathematical formulations.

**Numerical evidence**: In 10-fold CV (seed=13, `evidence_analysis.py`, Section 14), the 55 misclassified patients include 8 class-10 (RBBB) → class 1 errors — these patients likely need QRS width AND right axis deviation AND V1 morphology to be evaluated jointly for correct classification. CDS-OVR accumulates evidence from each feature independently, treating this conjunction as three features with moderate positive evidence rather than one strong combined signal. This is likely the primary reason CDS-OVR's 10-fold CV accuracy plateaus near 84–87% rather than exceeding 90%.

### 7.2 Continuous vs. Discrete Decision Boundaries

CDS-OVR's bins create step-function posteriors. Within a bin, all patients receive the same p_class estimate regardless of their exact feature values. A patient at QRS = 119.9 ms and another at QRS = 120.1 ms may fall in different bins and receive different evidence contributions, despite near-identical clinical presentations.

SVM and neural network decision boundaries are continuous functions of the input features, providing smoother and potentially more accurate classifications near decision boundaries.

### 7.3 End-to-End Optimization

CDS-OVR's staged pipeline (binning → posterior estimation → feature selection → threshold setting) optimizes each stage with a local objective. The chi-squared binning does not know the downstream effect of its split points on the ratio score. Neural networks and gradient-boosted trees optimize a single loss function end-to-end, producing globally coherent models.

The tradeoff: end-to-end optimization requires more data to avoid overfitting. CDS-OVR's staged optimization is more sample-efficient, which explains its strong performance at 90/10 where most classes have ≥10 training patients but the total dataset is too small for deep learning to fully generalize.

### 7.4 SMOTE / Synthetic Oversampling for Rare Classes

Irfan et al. apply SMOTE to the MIT-BIH dataset (D2) to balance class distributions before training. ### 7.5 Soft / Fuzzy Bin Boundaries

CDS-OVR assigns each feature value to exactly one bin, creating hard boundaries. A patient at 119.9 ms QRS duration receives entirely different evidence than one at 120.1 ms if a bin edge falls at 120 ms. Soft binning — where a value near a bin edge contributes partially to both adjacent bins — would produce smoother posterior estimates and reduce sensitivity to exact bin placement. This is analogous to the continuous decision boundaries that SVM and neural networks provide naturally.

### The Core Insight

CDS-OVR's class-aware specialization — the property that distinguishes it most fundamentally from all benchmarks — is simultaneously the source of its best performance and its primary limitation. When per-class data is sufficient (≥8–10 training patients per class, as at 90/10), specialization produces models tailored to each disease that outperform one-size-fits-all approaches. When per-class data falls below a critical threshold (~3 patients per bin), the specialized models become inoperative and the system loses its advantage.

This is not a flaw to be eliminated but a design tradeoff to be understood: CDS-OVR achieves its strong 90/10 and 10-fold CV results precisely because it specializes per-class, and that same specialization requires sufficient per-class data to produce meaningful posteriors. At 60/40, class 9 (LBBB) has only 4 training patients — the bins exist (87.5% usable) but the posteriors carry almost no signal. At 90/10, 8 training patients per class is sufficient for 2–3 bins to develop discriminative posteriors, enabling the model's structural advantages to activate.

### 8.1 Distinguishing Preprocessing Choices from Algorithmic Innovation

Many of CDS-OVR's advantages over benchmarks stem from *preprocessing decisions* (retaining all patients, keeping all features, stratifying by sex) rather than from the classification algorithm itself. These are important but should not be conflated with algorithmic superiority:

| Category | CDS-OVR Choice | What It Really Is |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Missing data handling | Skip NaN per-feature at prediction time | **Preprocessing choice** — any classifier could adopt this |
| Sex branching | Split population by feature 1 | **Preprocessing choice** — any pipeline could add this step |
| Retaining all 279 features as candidates | No column deletion | **Preprocessing choice** — preserves signal but requires per-feature NaN handling |
| Per-class feature selection (18 via FDR) | Class-specific Fisher scoring + correlation filter | **Algorithmic innovation** — requires OVR decomposition; cannot be trivially added to global classifiers |
| Dual AF with ratio scoring | Separate for/against evidence channels | **Algorithmic innovation** — fundamentally different inference mechanism from SVM margins or softmax |
| Dynamic healthy bar + suspicion | Patient-specific threshold adaptation | **Algorithmic innovation** — integrates healthy evidence into disease thresholds |
| Per-class against_scale | Imbalance-aware evidence weighting | **Algorithmic innovation** — encodes asymmetric confidence in positive vs. negative evidence for rare classes |

The honest assessment: if Sharma's RF were given the same preprocessing (no row deletion, no column deletion, per-class feature selection, sex stratification), it would likely close much of the accuracy gap. CDS-OVR's genuine algorithmic contributions — dual AF, healthy bar, per-class thresholds — are most impactful for sensitivity/specificity balance and rare-class detection, which are the clinically relevant metrics rather than raw accuracy.

The benchmark methods' advantages — feature interaction capture (SVM kernel, RF splits, CNN), end-to-end optimization (neural networks), principled regularization (L1/L2 penalties), calibrated probability outputs (LR, GNB), built-in variance reduction (RF bagging) — represent decades of validated ML research. CDS-OVR is a purpose-built system for this specific problem structure; it succeeds where domain-specific design choices align with dataset properties, and it lacks the generalization guarantees of established frameworks.

# Paper 1: A comparative study of heterogeneous machine learning algorithms for arrhythmia classification using feature selection technique and multidimensional datasets

Where CDS is stronger
1. Genuine multi-class architecture vs. binary collapse. The paper never actually solves the 13-class problem — it binarizes before training. CDS's OVR decomposition trains an independent evidence accumulator per class and combines them via per-class thresholds, which is architecturally suited to resolving which arrhythmia subtype a patient has, not just whether they have one. This is the single biggest structural advantage, since it's the harder and more clinically useful task.
2. Per-class handling of severe class imbalance. As established, RARE_CLASSES gets its own support/confidence/against-scale parameters. None of LR/LDA/GNB/SVM/RF/XGB in the paper have any per-class accommodation — they fit one global decision rule and would default toward majority-class behavior on 2–4-instance classes.
3. Target-aware feature scoring per class. CDS's _train_ovr_node searches all 279 features separately for each class using a class-specific shift/confidence/Fisher score. The paper's Pearson-correlation filter selects features based on correlation with other features, not the label — it's not even target-aware, let alone class-aware.
4. Native handling of missing data. branch_match returns False on NaN, and binning simply skips missing values feature-by-feature. The paper drops an entire 80%-missing column and then drops every row with any remaining missing value, shrinking 452 patients to 420 before a single model is trained. CDS retains the full 452 and lets each feature's model use whatever data exists for that feature.
5. No leakage in fold isolation. build_tree/train are rebuilt from scratch on training-fold-only data each CV iteration. The paper's feature selection happens before the split (Figure 1), which is exactly the mistake Ch. 11 of your Zollanvari reading quantified.
6. No preprocessing dependency. CDS's bin-based scoring is invariant to monotonic transforms of each feature — no normalization/scaling step is needed (contrast with Figure 4's explicit before/after normalization in the paper), and no imputation strategy has to be chosen and justified.
Where the paper's ML algorithms could be stronger
1. Battle-tested implementations vs. bespoke code. LR/RF/XGB/SVM in scikit-learn have been validated across thousands of studies and datasets; bugs get found and fixed by a huge user base. CDS is a ~600-line custom research script with a documented history of real bugs in this exact codebase (the prior-conflation bug, the partner's best-of-N-seeds selection bias). The prior probability of an undiscovered bug in CDS is meaningfully higher than in sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestClassifier.
2. Principled, systematic hyperparameter search vs. hand-tuned constants. Grid Search over a defined space (Table 23) — even though it's undermined here by pre-split leakage — is at least a documented, reproducible procedure. CDS's CLASS_THRESHOLDS, MIN_SUPPORT_MAP, CONF_SUPPORT_MAP, AGAINST_SCALE_MAP are hardcoded constants with a changelog in the docstring ("class 10 threshold = 3.0, lowered from 3.5") that reads like manual iteration against eval numbers rather than a defined search procedure with a held-out validation layer.
3. Far fewer degrees of freedom to overfit. LR has 3 hyperparameters (penalty, C, solver). CDS has per-class support/confidence/scale/threshold values — effectively dozens of tunable knobs across 13 classes, all fit (or hand-picked) against a 452-patient dataset. More knobs relative to sample size is a direct overfitting risk, independent of whether each individual mechanism is well-motivated.
4. Built-in variance reduction via ensembling. This is the one that connects directly to the split-variance discussion: Random Forest's bagging explicitly averages over many bootstrap-resampled trees specifically to reduce variance from which patients happen to be sampled. CDS builds exactly one deterministic tree per training set (build_tree isn't randomized or resampled at all) — there's no internal mechanism analogous to bagging that would dampen the kind of small-sample split sensitivity you saw in the 88.6%/97.6% gap. If instability across folds turns out to be a real problem for CDS under LOOCV, RF's architecture has a structural answer for it that CDS's doesn't.
5. Calibrated/free uncertainty estimates. LR and GNB output genuine class probabilities; RF gets out-of-bag error estimates without a separate CV loop. CDS's for:against ratio score is not a calibrated probability, and there's no analogous "free" internal validation signal — every uncertainty estimate CDS needs (LOOCV, seed-variance) has to be computed externally and by hand.
6. Unified regularization objective. XGB and LR regularize via an explicit penalty term in a single loss function (L1/L2), which is theoretically grounded and has one clean knob to tune. CDS's "regularization" is distributed across several separate ad hoc mechanisms (min support cutoffs, confidence caps, against-scale down-weighting, threshold offsets) with no unifying objective — harder to reason about what any one of them is actually trading off against the others.
The throughline: CDS's advantages are almost all about being purpose-built for this exact problem's structure (rare classes, multi-subtype, missing data). The paper's methods' advantages are almost all about being general-purpose tools with decades of validation and fewer ways to quietly go wrong. Neither list resolves the open question from before — whether CDS's purpose-built advantages actually pay off once you strip away seed selection and pre-split leakage on both sides is still what the LOOCV run would tell you.

# Paper 2: Classification of Arrhythmia Using Machine Learning Techniques

Where CDS is stronger
1. OneR's single-attribute ceiling is exactly the failure mode Holte's own paper predicts for this dataset. Holte's soybean example is the direct analogue: with 15 classes and no single attribute cleanly separating more than a handful of them, a 1-rule tops out around 86% even under ideal conditions — and that's the best case, assuming one attribute perfectly separates the largest classes. Holte's own diagnostic (his §2, "characteristic of the CH and SO datasets") is precisely "is there any attribute with more distinct values than there are classes?" For arrhythmia — 279 attributes, most either binary or supervised-binned into a handful of levels, against 13-16 classes with several at n<5 — that condition almost certainly fails. OneR's 56–61% in Table 1 is consistent with exactly this bound, not a training/implementation problem. CDS's OVR decomposition sidesteps this entirely: it never needs one attribute to do all the work, because each class gets its own multi-feature evidence accumulator (up to FEATURES_PER_CLASS=18 features).
2. J48's split criterion is majority-class-dominated by construction, which structurally starves rare classes. Witten & Frank are explicit: "a decision tree split takes all classes into account, trying to maximize the purity of the split" — information gain/gain ratio is computed by pooling all classes at a node. With Normal ECG at 245 and Right Bundle Branch Block at 50 dwarfing Supraventricular Premature Contraction at 2, the entropy reduction from any split is overwhelmingly driven by the big classes; there's little gain-based incentive to carve out a branch that isolates a 2-instance class. Compounding this, C4.5's default "minimum instances per branch outcome" is 2 (stated explicitly in the textbook) — meaning several of this dataset's classes sit right at the edge of the threshold below which a split is simply not created. Then postpruning compounds it again: the pessimistic error estimate is an upper confidence bound computed from training-set N and E, and small N produces wide confidence intervals and therefore high pessimistic error — which is exactly the condition that triggers pruning a subtree back to a leaf. So a rare class isn't just hard to split on; if it does get a branch, it's disproportionately likely to get pruned away afterward. This is also probably why the authors themselves describe J48's accuracy as "unstable" across splits (Table 1's 25-point range, 91.81% training-set-itself down to 67.03% at 80/20) while OneR and Naive Bayes are comparatively stable — J48's rare-class branches are exactly what's flickering in and out depending on which few instances land in which split.
CDS's answer: MIN_SUPPORT_MAP/CONF_SUPPORT_MAP explicitly lower the support bar for RARE_CLASSES rather than applying one global constant, and there's no pruning step that can eliminate a class's evidence accumulator entirely — every class always gets a shot regardless of how rare.
3. Naive Bayes's per-class Gaussian/frequency estimates get unstable exactly where it matters most. Langley et al.'s formal analysis is instructive here specifically because of what it doesn't model: their whole treatment assumes P(C)=½ (explicitly held constant "to normalize for effects of the base rate") and Boolean, independent, noise-free attributes. Their graceful-degradation result is real but it's derived under balanced classes — it says nothing about what happens when a class has 2–3 training instances, which is the actual regime several arrhythmia classes are in. With n=2, a per-class Gaussian variance estimate (which WEKA's Naive Bayes needs for continuous attributes) is close to degenerate. Interestingly, Witten & Frank's generalized Laplace/m-estimate — (count + m·p) / (N + m), where p is a prior probability the counts get shrunk toward — is the same formula as CDS's p_class = (target_counts + LAPLACE_ALPHA·prior) / (bin_counts + LAPLACE_ALPHA), just with p set to the node-local class prior rather than WEKA Naive Bayes's default uniform 1/k. That's a real point in CDS's favor: it's using the textbook's own "more principled" generalization of Laplace smoothing (which Witten & Frank note is harder to justify but more rigorous), while WEKA's default Naive Bayes uses the simpler add-1 version.
Where OneR, J48, and Naive Bayes could be stronger (or CDS's exposure the comparison reveals)
1. Missing-value handling is inconsistent across the three algorithms in this very paper, and the paper's own description glosses over it. Soman & Bobbie state blanket-style that "the instance with missing values is probabilistically assigned a possible value... by Weka," but per Holte, OneR "treats missing as a legitimate value" (a distinct category, not an imputation); per Witten & Frank, J48 does fractional-instance-weighting (splits an instance with a missing value into weighted pieces sent down every branch); and per Soman & Bobbie's own text, Naive Bayes "simply skips" unknown attributes. These are three different mechanisms, not one "Weka" behavior — the paper's description is imprecise about which algorithm does what. CDS's approach (skip NaN at the point of use, no imputation) is simpler and uniform across the whole pipeline, and doesn't inherit whatever bias each of these three distinct WEKA mechanisms introduces.
2. J48's rule-simplicity/interpretability advantage is real and CDS doesn't have an equivalent. Witten & Frank's pruned-rule extraction (§ "From trees to rules") produces a small set of human-readable IF-THEN rules with an explicit pessimistic-error-based simplification pass. Holte's whole "simplicity first" methodology argument is that a static-complexity/accuracy tradeoff exists and should be made visible, and OneR sits at the simple end of that tradeoff deliberately, as a benchmark. CDS has no equivalent artifact — its evidence-accumulator scores (af_for, af_against, Fisher-weighted feature contributions) are not something a clinician could read as a short rule list the way a pruned J48 tree or even a OneR 1-rule can be.
3. Naive Bayes's actual formal guarantee (Langley et al.) is a genuine strength the WEKA baselines have that CDS doesn't. Langley, Iba, and Thompson derive closed-form expected accuracy as a function of n, attribute count, and noise — an actual average-case theoretical guarantee, checked against experiment. There is no analogous formal characterization of CDS's behavior; its per-class threshold/support constants are empirically tuned, not derived. If Naive Bayes's assumptions held reasonably well here (a big if, given non-independence between many ECG features), its accuracy would be predictable in a way CDS's isn't.
4. 10-fold CV as the primary metric here is a step better than the Sharma paper's raw splits, but the split results reported alongside it undercut the paper's own reliability. Table 1 reports training-set-itself, 50/50, 70/30, and 80/20 in addition to what's presumably the 10-fold number — and the spread across these for the same algorithm (J48: 91.81 → 67.03, a 25-point swing) is larger than anything we saw in the Sharma paper's DS1–DS3. That's useful independent confirmation of the split-variance point from earlier, now visible in three algorithms' worth of data rather than one, and it strengthens the case that CDS's own eventual LOOCV numbers need to be read the same way — as the only member of that family of results actually resistant to this instability, not automatically superior to it.
The throughline is consistent with the earlier comparison: CDS's per-class accommodations are targeted, principled responses to specific mechanisms in OneR (single-attribute ceiling), J48 (majority-dominated splitting + pruning-away of small branches), and Naive Bayes (per-class parameter estimation with too little data) that are visible directly in these three source papers — while what CDS gives up is the maturity, interpretability, and (in Naive Bayes's case) formal theoretical grounding that these established methods bring.

# Paper 3: MODULAR NEURAL NETWORK BASED ARRHYTHMIA CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM USING ECG SIGNAL DATA

Where CDS is stronger
1. This paper's evaluation methodology has a genuine leakage problem, worse than anything seen so far. "DSMains" trains on all 452 instances and reports the same numbers as if they were performance results — 96.32%, 96.94%, 93.95% (Table 3) — but this is resubstitution error: training and "testing" on identical data. That's not a held-out estimate at all; it's an upper bound on how well the network memorized its own training set, and the paper reports it in the same table, at the same weight, as genuine train/test splits (DS1–DS5). Notice the pattern: DSMains's 93–97% dwarfs every genuine split's result (72–82%), which is exactly the gap you'd expect from overfitting rather than a genuinely better data configuration. CDS's fold-isolated retraining, whatever its other issues, never conflates "seen this data during training" with "evaluated on this data."
2. The missing-value imputation method may leak class-label information into the "unseen" data. "Missing attribute values... are replaced by closest column value of the concern class" — worded as: fill in a missing value using other instances of the same class. If this imputation is done before the train/test split (the paper never specifies otherwise, and it's described in §3.1 as a single blanket preprocessing step applied to "the entire database" before Data Set Groups are formed in §3.2), then a test instance's missing feature can be filled in using information derived partly from its own true label — which the model isn't supposed to have access to at evaluation time. This is a more serious version of the Sharma paper's pre-split feature-selection leak, because here it's literally using the target variable to construct an input feature. CDS never imputes at all — NaN is simply excluded from a feature's computation, with no dependency on any label.
3. No hyperparameter selection protocol, and results across depth are non-monotonic in an unexplained way. Sensitivity and specificity bounce inconsistently as hidden-layer count goes 1→2→3 within the same data set (e.g., DS1: sensitivity 73.68→65.79→71.05, specificity 82.69→84.62→84.62 — no clear trend), and the paper picks "2 hidden layers" as best largely by eyeballing Fig. 2/4 rather than any systematic search or held-out validation layer. Zhou's Ch. 5 treatment gives the likely explanation: backprop converges to different local minima depending on initialization, and with no mention of multiple restarts or seed control, this network's reported numbers per configuration are each a single draw from that variability — the same "single-run instability" problem flagged with CDS's own seed sensitivity, just undocumented here rather than measured. CDS at least has the virtue (however imperfectly exercised) of your own methodological rule requiring confirmation across many folds before trusting a result.
4. This paper also collapses to binary classification, same critique as Sharma: "these 15 classes are merged into a single class called 'Abnormal'" (§3.1). The 99.02% figure cited for Niwas et al. in the related-work section (§2) is for the genuinely harder 9-class task and is flagged by the authors themselves as "only for few classes" — an implicit admission that their own binary framing sidesteps the harder multi-class problem CDS is actually attempting.
Where the neural network approach could be stronger
1. Universal approximation is a real theoretical property CDS's tree-plus-Bayesian-evidence architecture doesn't have. Per Zhou's Ch. 5 (Cybenko, 1989), a three-layer MLP can approximate arbitrary decision regions arbitrarily well given enough hidden units — a formal expressiveness guarantee. CDS's evidence-accumulator design is a fixed functional form (weighted for/against ratios); there's no equivalent theorem saying it can represent an arbitrary decision boundary given enough features or nodes. If the true class boundaries in this dataset are highly nonlinear and don't decompose cleanly into per-feature bin-level evidence, an MLP has a structural capacity CDS lacks.
2. Regularization mechanisms are principled and battle-tested, versus CDS's ad hoc constants. Zollanvari's Ch. 13 covers dropout with a specific probabilistic justification (train-time random unit omission approximating an ensemble, with the (1-p) test-time rescaling to match expected activation) and early stopping tied to monitored validation metrics. This paper doesn't actually use either (no dropout or early-stopping callback is mentioned — training presumably runs to convergence or a fixed epoch count via NeuroSolutions defaults), which is itself a weakness relative to what the architecture could offer if used properly. But the underlying mechanism — a single global regularization knob rather than CDS's fragmented per-class threshold system — is at least a coherent, well-understood one when it is used.
3. Modularity is a real, if underexplained, structural idea that parallels CDS's OVR decomposition. The "Modular" in MNN suggests sub-networks handling different aspects of the classification, which is philosophically similar to CDS's per-class evidence accumulators — decompose a hard multi-way problem into more tractable sub-problems. Unfortunately the paper never actually describes how the modules are organized or combined (Fig. 1 just shows a generic multilayer diagram, no module boundaries drawn), so this is a strength the paper claims but doesn't substantiate — worth noting as a gap in the paper itself rather than a real point of comparison until better documented.
4. Backprop's gradient-based optimization scales to more features/parameters than CDS's greedy per-feature scoring. CDS scores each of 279 features independently per node per class (no interaction terms beyond what correlation-filtering removes); an MLP's hidden layer can in principle learn feature combinations jointly via gradient descent across the whole network simultaneously, rather than each feature being evaluated in isolation the way _train_ovr_node's scoring loop does. Whether that theoretical advantage materializes with only 452 patients and no dropout/regularization shown in this paper is a separate question — but the optimization framework itself is more expressive in principle.
Bottom line on this one specifically: the paper's biggest weaknesses (DSMains resubstitution reported as a result, likely label-leaking imputation, no seed/init control) are methodological rather than about the neural network architecture itself — so this comparison mostly reinforces the same lesson as the earlier two papers rather than adding a fundamentally new axis. The genuine architectural strengths of neural networks (universal approximation, joint feature-interaction learning via gradient descent) are real and worth taking seriously, but this particular paper doesn't demonstrate them convincingly given how it's evaluated.

Kian's Note: The "strengths" for the paper as outlined by Claude are guesses at best, considering the original paper does not mention how general aspects of neural networks, which was covered in typical machine learning textbooks, have benefitted the accurate diagnosis of arrhythmia is significant ways.

Comparing 88% (this chapter, single stratified split) against 86.6% (Voleti & Reddi, 10-fold CV), 84% (Anderies), and 85% (Grandhi & Singh, "stratified cross-validation") without controlling for evaluation protocol, class-imbalance handling, or dataset version repeats exactly the benchmarking flaw already discussed — small differences in accuracy across studies using different train/test protocols aren't meaningfully comparable, and CDS's eventual LOOCV number, once obtained, would be more directly comparable to none of these four than to each other.
Where the four combined ML models could be stronger than CDS
1. AdaBoost's boosting mechanism is a more principled ensemble strategy than CDS's ad hoc evidence weighting. AdaBoost reweights training examples adaptively based on prior misclassifications, with a formally derived exponential-loss-minimizing combination rule (Zhou's Ch. 8 material from earlier in this conversation covers the derivation). CDS's _compute_af also produces a weighted combination of per-feature "votes" (Fisher-weighted, confidence-scaled), but the weights are hand-set constants (support/confidence thresholds, against-scale) rather than something learned from training error the way AdaBoost's example-reweighting is. AdaBoost's 69% here is actually the weakest of the four in this chapter, but that's likely an artifact of running it on 279 raw/lightly-filtered features without the kind of per-class rare-class accommodation CDS has — the underlying boosting mechanism itself is a more rigorously justified way to combine weak signal than CDS's static weighting scheme.
2. SVM's margin-maximization gives a formal generalization guarantee that CDS's threshold-based decision rule doesn't have. SVM's 88%/100%/82.75% here is the best of the four, and structurally SVM is optimizing for the maximum-margin separating hyperplane — a criterion tied to VC-dimension-based generalization bounds (from the computational learning theory material covered earlier). CDS's predict() — comparing a for:against ratio against a set of hand-picked, per-class threshold constants — has no comparable theoretical guarantee that its decision boundary generalizes well; the thresholds were arrived at by iteration against reported accuracy, not derived from a margin or risk-bound argument.
3. KNN's instance-based approach makes no parametric assumptions and needs no per-node binning. CDS relies on binning each continuous feature into a handful of intervals (via _supervised_bin_edges) to compute class-conditional evidence — a discretization step that discards some information and introduces boundary-effect artifacts. KNN instead uses raw distance in feature space directly; for continuous physiological measurements like QRS duration or QT interval, that's arguably a more natural fit to the data's actual structure than CDS's chi-square-driven bin boundaries, which can be unstable with the same small-sample problem affecting rare classes (few examples means unstable optimal split points).
4. Decision Tree here at least attempts a genuinely data-driven multi-feature split structure, where CDS's tree is fixed and shallow. As established earlier, CDS's build_tree only ever splits on sex (feats = [SEX_FEAT] is hardcoded) and stops at depth 2. A standard decision tree, by contrast, searches all features at every node and can grow to whatever depth the data supports. Even acknowledging J48/CART's rare-class vulnerabilities discussed with the Soman & Bobbie paper, the DT here is at least structurally capable of discovering feature interactions CDS's fixed sex-only split can never find, since CDS's only structural (non-Bayesian-evidence) branching decision is hardcoded rather than learned from data.
5. All four benefit from being implemented in mature, widely-used libraries (the chapter names Anaconda/Python 3.8.5, implying scikit-learn), carrying the same "battle-tested implementation" advantage over CDS's bespoke code discussed with the Sharma comparison — worth restating here since it applies uniformly across all four models in this chapter too.
The pattern holds across every UCI-dataset paper we've now covered: the binary-collapse and single/small-split evaluation choices are the actual source of these papers' apparent performance advantage over CDS's harder, more rigorously-evaluated task — while the genuine architectural strengths (AdaBoost's adaptive reweighting, SVM's margin guarantee, KNN's non-parametric fit, unconstrained tree search) are real and worth CDS eventually being benchmarked against fairly, once both sides are run under the same protocol.

# Paper 5: Multiclass Classification of Cardiac Arrhythmia Using Improved Feature Selection and SVM Invariants

Where CDS is stronger
1. Same pre-split feature-selection leakage as every other paper reviewed, now compounded by data duplication. §3.1's WFS procedure is explicitly run before the train/test split described in §3.3 ("the normalized and filtered dataset is partitioned into training and testing sets" comes after feature selection). Worse, the WFS step itself creates "at least five copies of each data instance... resulting in an increase in the number of records to a total of 2260" to train the shadow-attribute-comparison Random Forest — meaning near-duplicates of whatever ends up in the eventual test set are present during feature selection. This is a more aggressive version of the leakage already flagged in Sharma et al. and Kumar et al., since it's not just information leaking across the split, it's literal near-duplicate rows of test instances present during the selection step. CDS's fold-isolated retraining has neither issue.
2. Missing-value imputation (column mean) again appears to be computed on the full dataset before splitting, same pattern as Jadhav's closest-column-value approach and Devadas's MICE — another spot where the eventual test set's own values likely influence what gets imputed for training rows (and vice versa), since §3.1 describes it as a single preprocessing pass prior to the described §3.3 split. CDS never imputes.
3. Feature selection is one global set shared across all 16 classes, not class-aware — the same majority-class-domination problem flagged for J48's gain ratio, now applying to Random-Forest permutation importance. WFS scores features by average accuracy-loss-on-permutation across RF's decision trees — an importance measure necessarily dominated by whichever features help discriminate the numerically largest classes, since RF's bagged trees are fit on the overall class distribution. A feature uniquely diagnostic of a 2- or 3-instance class has little chance of showing up in the global top-94 rankings this way. CDS's _train_ovr_node scores every feature separately per class, so a feature can be selected for class 9 even if it's globally uninformative for the dataset as a whole.
4. The paper's own Table 1 is a clean, self-supplied demonstration of the split-size inflation problem this conversation keeps surfacing. OAO's accuracy climbs monotonically as test-set size shrinks: 73.45% (50/50) → 74.59% (60/40) → 77.20% (70/30) → 81.11% (80/20) → 92.07% (90/10). That's an 18.6-point spread purely as a function of how much data is held out, on the same algorithm, same features, same run. This is the single clearest illustration across everything you've uploaded of why a hold-out percentage isn't a property of the model.
Where this paper's SVM-decomposition approach is genuinely stronger (or at least more self-aware) than papers reviewed earlier — and than CDS in places
1. To their credit, the authors don't report the flattering number as their headline result. After showing the monotonic climb in Table 1, they explicitly write: "data overfitting occurs when large amount of data is used for training, so a standard 80/20 data split is used for further implementation" — and then use 81.11% (not 92.07%) for every subsequent comparison (Tables 2 and 3). That's a real methodological step up from Sharma et al., who reported their most-inflated 90/10 number (95.24%) as the headline result without this caveat. It doesn't fix the underlying pre-split leakage, but it shows awareness of one of the two problems (split-size sensitivity) that this conversation has been tracking.
2. OAA is structurally the same family of strategy as CDS's own OVR decomposition — a genuinely apples-to-apples architectural comparison. CDS's train() builds one evidence accumulator per class against the rest — that's OAA (one-against-all) by definition. This paper runs OAA directly and reports it losing to OAO (n(n-1)/2 pairwise binary classifiers, 92.07%/81.11%) at every split size in Table 1. That's a directly relevant empirical data point: on this dataset, decomposing into all-pairs binary problems outperformed one-vs-rest decomposition, for SVM specifically. Whether that generalizes to CDS's very different underlying binary-decision mechanism (margin-maximizing hyperplane vs. Bayesian-evidence-ratio) is an open question, but it's a legitimate reason to ask whether CDS's chosen decomposition (OVR) is actually the best one for this dataset's structure, rather than assuming OVR is neutral.
3. Feature-selection method comparison (Table 3) is a genuinely useful ablation CDS doesn't have an equivalent for. The paper directly compares its wrapper method (81.11%) against PCA (76.67%), correlation-based selection (60.00%), and information-gain selection (68.89%) — all under the same split, same classifier, same everything else. That's a controlled ablation isolating the effect of feature-selection method alone. CDS has never been run with an alternative feature-scoring approach substituted in for comparison; we don't actually know how much of CDS's behavior is attributable to its specific shift/confidence/Fisher scoring versus what a simpler or more sophisticated selector would produce holding everything else fixed.
4. Kappa statistic and RMSE alongside accuracy give a more complete picture than accuracy alone. Kappa corrects for the accuracy a random or majority-class predictor would achieve by chance, which is particularly relevant given the class-imbalance concerns raised throughout this conversation — a model could post a deceptively high raw accuracy on a skewed 16-class problem just by defaulting to Normal ECG often enough. CDS's stats() function currently reports accuracy/specificity/sensitivity/balanced-accuracy but not kappa; adding it would make CDS's eventual LOOCV results more directly comparable to this paper's Table 2/3, and would separately flag if CDS's HEALTHY-biased fallback design (discussed earlier) is inflating accuracy without a matching kappa improvement.
5. TSVM's poor showing (49.74%) is an implicit warning about tuning-dependence that applies to CDS's own hand-set constants. The authors note TSVM "does not give optimal results" despite trying a defined grid over c1, c2, kernel p, and tolerance — a sophisticated architecture underperforming simpler OAO/OAA/ECC because of parameter sensitivity on a small, imbalanced dataset. That's a cautionary data point for CDS too: CLASS_THRESHOLDS, MIN_SUPPORT_MAP, etc. are hand-set rather than grid-searched at all, so there's no equivalent evidence yet about how sensitive CDS's results are to those specific constant choices versus nearby alternatives.
Bottom line: this is the closest genuine architectural peer to CDS you've found — real 16-class task, an OVR-family baseline (OAA) run directly, and (uniquely among the papers reviewed) explicit acknowledgment of the split-size inflation problem. The leakage issues (pre-split WFS with duplicated rows, pre-split imputation) mean 81.11%/92.07% still can't be taken as clean ground truth, but the paper's OAA-vs-OAO result and its feature-selection ablation are both directly actionable comparisons once CDS has a fair (LOOCV, leakage-free) number to put next to them.

This also sets a concrete bar for what CDS's own eventual LOOCV rare-class numbers need to look like to be trustworthy. Under true LOOCV, a class with 2–4 total instances will still only ever contribute 2–4 individual pass/fail data points to its balanced-accuracy estimate — structurally similar to this paper's tiny test counts. The difference is that LOOCV (unlike this paper's single 60/40 split) evaluates every instance of every rare class exactly once as a genuinely held-out case, with no PCA/scaler fit crossing the boundary — so if CDS's rare classes come back at 100% under LOOCV, that result would actually mean something, whereas this paper's superficially identical 100% rows do not.
